Walking around my classroom in Shanghai, it's easy to be dazzled by the tech. Every student has a high-spec laptop, the whiteboards are all interactive, and the school's "Learning Management System" is a masterpiece of engineering. But sometimes I look at a room full of children staring at screens and I wonder if we’ve accidentally digitised the soul out of the lesson.

## The Broad Debate

The debate about technology in schools usually boils down to two extremes. On one side are the "Tech Evangelists" who think a classroom without 1:1 iPads is living in the Stone Age. On the other are the "Luddites" who want to ban every screen and return to the days of chalk and slate. 

Both sides have a point, but they’re both missing the *realpolitik* of how children actually learn. Technology isn't the problem; it’s the **location** that’s wrong.

## What the Thinkers Say

The polite, moderate consensus—the kind you’d hear in a high-level educational forum—is that we should "integrate" technology into every aspect of the curriculum. The idea is that "digital literacy" is a core skill and that screens should be a seamless part of the classroom experience.

Again, this feels like a view from a privileged bubble. It assumes that thirty children on laptops will stay focused on the "inquiry-based task" rather than Minecraft or group chats. It ignores the messiness of classroom management. Most tech evangelists speak as if a screen is just a digital piece of paper, but any teacher knows a screen is actually a distraction machine.

## My View

My view is that we need to be much more intentional about the boundaries. We should keep the classroom as a purely human, analogue space—pen, paper, and face-to-face debate. But we should give the AI to the kids to use at home for their homework. Use the technology where its power for personalisation and marking actually shines, and keep the classroom for the one thing AI can't do: genuine social connection.

## Why the Split Works

### The Focus of Pen and Paper
When students write by hand in class, something changes. They process information more slowly and more deeply. More importantly, they are *present*. When I have a class with their laptops closed and their notebooks open, we have real debates. We listen to each other. We look at the whiteboard, not our own little private worlds. It's about preserving a shared space for learning.

### The Power of AI at Home
Homework is where the "Tech Evangelists" are right. Traditionally, homework is a one-size-fits-all worksheet that bores the bright kids and frustrates the struggling ones. This is where AI is brilliant. An adaptive algorithm can give each child exactly the right level of challenge at home. It can mark their work instantly and give me a data dashboard on Monday morning so I know exactly who didn't understand the algebra. 

## What Happens Next?

If we want to get the balance right, we should stop trying to "integrate" and start trying to "separate":
1. **Analogue Classrooms**: Try a "laptop-free" week. Focus on speaking, listening, and handwriting. See how the energy in the room shifts.
2. **Digital Homework**: Stop setting paper worksheets. Use platforms that actually track progress and provide instant feedback. 
3. **The Data Bridge**: The only tech you need in class is the teacher’s screen, showing the data from last night’s AI homework so you can target your teaching.

For parents and teachers: Don't feel guilty for wanting to ban screens in class. You're not being "old-fashioned"; you're being realistic about how humans actually connect. Keep the AI for the mechanical work at home, and keep the classroom for the human work.
