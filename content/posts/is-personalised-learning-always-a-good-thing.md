In the international school world, "Personalised Learning" is the golden ticket. The idea is that every student should have a curriculum tailored exactly to their pace, their interests, and their ability level. With AI, this is now actually possible. But lately, as I watch my students in Shanghai each plug into their own individualised "learning pathways," I’ve started to wonder if we’re accidentally destroying the community that makes a school a school.

## The Broad Debate

The debate usually pits the "Silicon Valley" types—who think education is just a series of skills to be "unlocked" at your own pace—against the "Traditionalists" who think there is a core body of knowledge that everyone should learn together. 

One side values efficiency and individual mastery; the other values cohesion and a shared culture. Both are right, but they are often talking past each other.

## What the Thinkers Say

The polite, moderate consensus (the "Rory and Alastair" vibe) is that we can have it both ways. They speak about "blended learning"—where students spend part of the day on their own and part of the day in a group. They assume that as long as the "outcomes" are good, the social fragmentation doesn't really matter.

But this seems like a view from a bubble where everyone already shares a common culture and a set of polite norms. It ignores the realpolitik of modern society. If children grow up only ever learning at their own pace, on their own terms, and according to their own interests, are we actually preparing them for a world that demands compromise and collective action?

## My View

My view is that personalisation is a brilliant tool for **skills**, but a disaster for **culture**. We should use AI to personalise things like maths drills or grammar practice—the mechanical stuff. But we should never personalise the "humanities." A class should read the same book, look at the same history, and debate the same ideas together. We need to preserve the classroom as a place of "shared gravity."

## Skills vs. Soul

### The Efficiency of Personalised Skills
Let's be honest: in an average classroom, a third of the kids are bored because it’s too easy, and a third are lost because it’s too hard. For things like coding or arithmetic, that's a waste of everyone's time. This is where personalisation is a dream. It lets the struggling student get the extra practice they need without being embarrassed, and it lets the high-flyers get ahead.

### The Necessity of Shared Experience
But imagine a history lesson where every student is reading a different article about a different event, tailored to their "interest." We would have 30 individuals, but no class. Education isn't just about "learning things"; it's about learning to be part of an "us." If we don't all struggle with the same difficult text together, we lose the shared frame of reference that allows us to have a conversation in the first place.

## What Happens Next?

If you're a parent or a teacher, try to draw a clear line:
1. **Personalise the Tools**: Use adaptive platforms for the "drills"—maths, languages, vocab. This is where the machine wins.
2. **Standardise the Ideas**: Keep the whole-class novel, the shared history topic, and the group science project. Force the kids to engage with the same material at the same time.
3. **Bring back the Debate**: The most "personalised" thing you can do for a student is to listen to their individual opinion about a shared problem. That’s where the "human" personalisation actually happens.

For schools: Don't let the tech gurus convince you that a room full of children with headphones on is a "modern classroom." It’s an office. Keep the personalised pathways for home, and keep the classroom for the shared, messy, human work of learning together.
