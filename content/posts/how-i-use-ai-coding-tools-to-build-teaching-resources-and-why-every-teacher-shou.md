Most teachers I know use AI in a very similar way: they open ChatGPT, type "write me a worksheet about persuasive writing," and then spend twenty minutes copy-pasting the output into a Word document and fixing the formatting. It's useful, and it definitely saves time, but I've started to realise that it's only about 5% of what's actually possible.

## The Broad Debate

There's a growing divide in how we talk about tech in the staffroom. On one side are the "traditionalists" who feel that AI-generated resources are a bit soulless and that we should stick to tried-and-tested textbooks. On the other are the "tech optimists" who think that "prompting" is the new literacy and that we should all be masters of the chat box.

But both of these views feel a bit limited to me. One ignores the sheer power of the tool, and the other treats it like a magic genie that you just have to "ask" nicely.

## What the Thinkers Say

The current chatter in my "polite, privileged bubble" of international education is all about "Prompt Engineering." The idea is that if you just learn the right sequence of words, the AI will give you exactly what you need. People like Rory Stewart or Alastair Campbell might look at this and see a civil service dream—efficiency, standardisation, and better management.

But Sitting here in Shanghai, watching how actual coders work, I’ve realised that "prompting" is actually a very brittle way to work. If you change one word in your prompt, the whole output shifts. It’s not a system; it’s a conversation. And conversations are hard to scale.

## My View

My view is that the real "superpower" for teachers isn't prompting—it's **vibe coding**. This is a term coined by Andrej Karpathy to describe a way of working where you describe what you want, and the AI builds the *code* to generate it. You aren't asking for a worksheet; you're building a machine that can produce thirty different, perfectly-formatted booklets in ten minutes. It’s moving from being a "writer of content" to a "builder of systems."

## Why "Systems" are Better than "Prompts"

### Total Consistency
When I use an AI coding tool (like Antigravity or Cursor) to write a Python script for my resources, every single worksheet I produce follows the exact same style. I don’t have to worry about font sizes or margin errors. The "coder" handles the formatting, so I just have to focus on the curriculum.

### Data-Driven Differentiation
This is where it gets really exciting. Because I'm using code, I can feed in a spreadsheet of my students' last test results. The script can then generate different versions of the same booklet—one for the kids who struggled with Topic A, and one for the kids who need a challenge. Doing this manually would take a weekend; doing it with a script takes an afternoon.

## What Happens Next?

If you want to move beyond the chat box, you don't actually need to learn how to code. You just need to change how you talk to the AI:
1. **Don't ask for a worksheet**: Ask the AI to "Write a Python script that turns this list of vocab words into a 5-page PDF with definitions, gap-fills, and an answer key."
2. **Use Markdown**: Keep your ideas in simple text files. It's the language of coders and machines, and it makes it much easier for the AI to understand your "vibe."
3. **Build a Archive**: Once you have a script that works for one unit, you have it for life. Next year, you just run it again with new words.

For teachers: Don't be intimidated by the word "coding." If you can describe what you want, you can build it. We have the chance to move from being under-resourced managers to being architects of our own curriculum. Why wouldn't we take it?
